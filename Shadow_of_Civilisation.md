# **Shadows, Contagions, and Moral Boundaries**

## **A Visual Series on Collective Psychology, Civilization, and Technology**

This infographic series explores recurring patterns in human psychology and civilization: projection, moral panic, dehumanization, ideological possession, purity myths, and technological anxiety. Drawing from psychology, philosophy, anthropology, media theory, and history, the series examines how societies construct symbolic enemies, spread fear through crowds and networks, define moral boundaries, and react to transformative technologies.

The project emerged from a simple observation: although civilizations change, many of the underlying psychological structures remain remarkably consistent. The same mechanisms that once fueled witch hunts, racial hierarchies, ideological purges, and moral panics continue to operate in modern media ecologies, online outrage cycles, political tribalism, and debates surrounding artificial intelligence.

Each infographic focuses on a distinct but interconnected theme:

- **The Shadow** — projection, repression, and symbolic enemies.

- **Moral Panic & Memetic Contagion** — how fear spreads through crowds and networks.

- **The Psychology of Totalitarianism** — ideology, conformity, and the collapse of truth.

- **The Myth of Purity** — cleansing narratives, pollution myths, and sacrificial exclusion.

- **The Denial of the Other** — dehumanization, moral exclusion, and the expanding moral circle.

- **Sacred vs Demonic Technology** — mythic responses to technological transformation.

The series draws on thinkers such as Carl Jung, Hannah Arendt, René Girard, Mary Douglas, Marshall McLuhan, Lewis Mumford, Frantz Fanon, Simone de Beauvoir, Martin Buber, Erich Fromm, Gustave Le Bon, and Peter Singer, combining insights from psychology, philosophy, anthropology, media theory, and history.

Rather than reducing complex events to simplistic ideological narratives, the project seeks to illuminate deeper structural patterns:

- how fear becomes contagious,

- how moral certainty can override empathy,

- how societies repeatedly define outsiders as less than fully human,

- and how new technologies become mirrors for both utopian hopes and apocalyptic anxieties.

A central theme running through the series is the question of recognition: who counts as fully human, fully conscious, or fully worthy of moral concern? Across history, these boundaries have expanded unevenly and often painfully — from race and gender to animals and, increasingly, questions surrounding artificial intelligence and digital minds.

The goal of the series is neither cynicism nor moral relativism. It is an attempt to cultivate psychological and civilizational self-awareness: to better understand the forces that shape collective behavior, and to encourage forms of culture capable of resisting dehumanization, hysteria, and ideological possession.

Technology amplifies human possibility. The direction civilization takes remains a human choice.
